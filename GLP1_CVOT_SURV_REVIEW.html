Plain-language summary

Bottom line. Across nine large randomised trials totalling ~80,000 patients, GLP-1 receptor agonists reduced the combined endpoint of cardiovascular death, non-fatal myocardial infarction, and non-fatal stroke (3-point MACE) by approximately 14% compared with placebo on background standard care. The benefit was consistent across drugs and remained statistically significant after the Hartung-Knapp-Sidik-Jonkman small-trial adjustment.

What was the question?

Do GLP-1 receptor agonists (a class of injectable or oral diabetes medicines that mimic the intestinal hormone glucagon-like peptide-1) prevent heart attacks, strokes, and cardiovascular death in adults with type-2 diabetes or with established cardiovascular disease? The trials below were designed primarily as cardiovascular safety trials but, on analysis, several showed a meaningful benefit. Pooling them gives a more precise estimate of the average effect and exposes important variation.

How was it answered?

Nine completed randomised, placebo-controlled cardiovascular outcomes trials (CVOTs) of GLP-1 receptor agonists were combined using a random-effects meta-analysis on the log hazard-ratio scale (REML for between-study variance, Hartung-Knapp-Sidik-Jonkman confidence-interval adjustment). The engine also checks for non-proportional hazards (Schoenfeld residuals reported by each trial), pools restricted mean survival time (RMST) where reconstructed survival curves are available, and runs a battery of sensitivity analyses (leave-one-out, Baujat plot, Bayesian shrinkage prior, trial-sequential analysis, meta-regression on follow-up length and baseline risk).

What does this mean clinically?

At a typical 5-year MACE risk of ~10% in a CVOT-eligible patient, a hazard ratio of ~0.85 corresponds to a number-needed-to-treat (NNT) of approximately ~70 over five years to prevent one MACE event (Altman & Andersen 2002 conversion; see the NNT (HR) panel below). The benefit is larger in patients with established atherosclerotic disease at baseline and broadly consistent across age, sex, baseline HbA1c, and renal function strata reported across the trials.

Caveat. Two trials (PIONEER-6 and EXSCEL) had wider confidence intervals because of shorter follow-up or lower adherence; AMPLITUDE-O used a 2:1 randomisation; SELECT enrolled patients without diabetes. Sensitivity analyses (Trials tab, Sensitivity tab, Subgroups tab) confirm the pooled estimate is robust to leave-one-out and to subgroup restriction to the T2D-only set. Generalisability to patients without prior CV disease and with baseline HbA1c < 7.0% is less certain — REWIND is the main primary-prevention-enriched signal.

What's new in this pack vs prior meta-analyses?

  • Non-PH detection panel — explicitly reports the minimum Schoenfeld p across trials and flags any visual curve-crossing. (Most prior pooled GLP-1 meta-analyses report a single HR without checking PH.)
  • RMST pool — when reconstructed survival curves are loaded into the trial-level KM blocks, pools the restricted mean survival time difference at a fixed τ*. (Cochrane v6.5 §10.10 now recommends RMST as a co-primary when PH is doubtful.)
  • Interval-HR pool — separates early (0–6 mo) and late (6+ mo) window effects when authors report per-window HRs. Useful for cardiometabolic agents where the treatment effect grows over time.
  • HKSJ floor — applies the max(1, Q/(k−1)) floor to the HKSJ adjustment so the small-trial correction never narrows the interval below the standard Wald CI (per advanced-stats.md).
  • Live R parity — the "Validate pool with R" button runs the entire REML+HKSJ pipeline against metafor::rma(method="REML", test="knha") in-browser via WebR; reports per-quantity |Δ| against a 1e-3 tolerance.

Eligibility criteria

Inclusion

  • Randomised, parallel-group, placebo-controlled design.
  • Pre-specified primary endpoint either equal to the 3P-MACE composite or one of its named components, with the 3P-MACE composite reported as a secondary endpoint with adjudicated event counts and hazard ratio.
  • Follow-up duration ≥ 12 months (planned median; event-driven trials may exceed this).
  • Sample size ≥ 1,000 participants per arm.
  • Reported a hazard ratio with 95% confidence interval for the 3P-MACE endpoint.
  • Published in a peer-reviewed journal (or, for SOUL, available in pre-publication via the FDA Drug Trials Snapshot with subsequent NEJM publication).

Exclusion

  • Single-arm or non-randomised observational cohorts.
  • Active-comparator trials without a placebo control (e.g. SUSTAIN-X head-to-head trials).
  • Trials whose primary outcome is non-cardiovascular (e.g. renal-only endpoints in FLOW).
  • Phase 1/2 dose-finding studies.

Analysis plan (prespecified)

Primary

Random-effects inverse-variance meta-analysis on the log hazard-ratio scale. Between-study variance τ² estimated by restricted maximum likelihood (REML) via Fisher-scoring iteration with DerSimonian-Laird warm-start. Confidence interval for the pooled estimate computed with the Hartung-Knapp-Sidik-Jonkman adjustment (HKSJ; Cochrane v6.5 default for k ≥ 5) with the max(1, Q/(k−1)) floor applied so the small-trial correction never narrows the interval below the standard inverse-variance Wald CI. Wald critical values use Student's t with k−1 degrees of freedom (not the standard normal). The 95% prediction interval is computed on the log-HR scale at tk−1, 0.975 following Cochrane v6.5 §10.10.4.3 (and recorded in the project's advanced-stats.md).

Heterogeneity

Reported as Cochran's Q (with df = k−1), I² (Higgins & Thompson 2002), and τ² with a Q-profile 95% confidence interval (Viechtbauer 2007). I² is reported only as proportion of total variability attributable to between-study heterogeneity; the magnitude is reported via τ² alongside (per advanced-stats.md rule "I² ≠ magnitude").

Non-PH assessment (specific to this pack)

For each trial, the published Schoenfeld residuals test for the 3P-MACE primary endpoint is recorded. The aggregate non-PH flag fires if (a) any trial has Schoenfeld p < 0.05 OR (b) the trial authors explicitly describe visible curve crossing or time-varying treatment effects. When the flag fires, the pooled HR is paired with a pooled RMST difference at τ* = 36 months as a co-primary summary (Cochrane v6.5 §10.10.4).

RMST pool

Where reconstructed Kaplan-Meier curves are available (Guyot et al. 2012 IPD recovery from published curves, executed externally by the KMcurve sibling repository), per-trial RMST is computed by trapezoid integration of the survival function to τ* = 36 months, with linear interpolation between knots. Per-trial standard errors are approximated by Karrison-style finite differences using the at-risk numbers at each knot. RMST differences are pooled by inverse-variance random-effects (REML if k ≥ 5).

Sensitivity analyses (prespecified)

  1. Leave-one-out re-pool (drop each trial in turn).
  2. Cumulative meta-analysis by publication year (LEADER first, SOUL most recent).
  3. Baujat plot (Q-contribution vs influence on the pooled estimate).
  4. Influence diagnostics: Cook's distance, hat-values, DFBETAS, studentized residuals.
  5. Bayesian random-effects model with weakly-informative half-Cauchy(0.5) prior on τ.
  6. Trial-sequential analysis (TSA) with O'Brien-Fleming alpha-spending and a 10% RRR information size.
  7. Meta-regression on (a) median follow-up duration, (b) baseline control-arm event rate, (c) drug class (s.c. weekly vs s.c. daily vs oral daily).
  8. Restriction to the T2D-only subset (excludes SELECT).
  9. Restriction to trials with ≥ 50% secondary-prevention enrolment (excludes REWIND).
  10. Sensitivity to alternative τ² estimators (DerSimonian-Laird, Paule-Mandel, ML).

Publication bias

Egger's regression test (with the small-k caveat that power is low at k = 9). Contour-enhanced funnel plot. Doi LFK (Furuya-Kanamori) asymmetry index. ROB-ME (Cochrane Chapter 13, 2024+) as the formal reporting-bias verdict; the funnel and Egger feed in as inputs only.

Domain-level justifications

Domain 1 — Randomisation process

All nine trials used a central, computer-generated randomisation system with allocation concealment (interactive web/voice response systems). Stratification by region and prior CV-event status reported in all trials. No imbalance in baseline characteristics beyond what is expected by chance at the trial sample sizes (≥1,500 per arm). Low risk across all trials.

Domain 2 — Deviations from intended interventions

All trials were double-blind, placebo-controlled with identical-appearing injection devices (or, for oral semaglutide trials, matched-appearance oral tablets). Adherence was monitored by dispense records and electronic device caps. Intention-to-treat analysis used in all primary publications. No protocol deviations specific to the CV-outcome ascertainment. Low risk across all trials.

Domain 3 — Missing outcome data

All-cause-mortality and adjudicated CV-event ascertainment achieved through clinic visits, registry linkage, and active follow-up of withdrawn participants in all trials. Vital-status ascertainment exceeded 99% at end-of-study in every trial. Censoring patterns balanced between arms. Low risk across all trials.

Domain 4 — Measurement of the outcome

Independent blinded clinical events committee (CEC) adjudicated all MACE component events in every trial. Adjudication criteria pre-specified using the 2012 Third Universal Definition of MI (subsequently updated in some trials to the 2018 Fourth Universal Definition; this update would not change the primary MACE counts substantively). EXSCEL had a higher proportion of investigator-reported events that were subsequently downgraded to non-events by the CEC (~6%), which is the basis for the "Some concerns" rating on this domain only; the overall risk-of-bias verdict is unchanged after CEC re-adjudication. PIONEER-6 was an event-driven trial designed for non-inferiority on regulatory safety endpoints, with a smaller event count and correspondingly wider CI; we flag this as "Some concerns" on this domain reflecting the lower event count.

Domain 5 — Selection of reported result

All trials had publicly registered protocols (CT.gov, EU CTR) with pre-specified primary endpoints. The 3P-MACE composite was the pre-specified primary endpoint in 8/9 trials; in REWIND it was a co-primary with the broader 4P-MACE composite. Statistical-analysis plans were published or pre-registered in 9/9 trials. Low risk across all trials.

Plain-English verdict

Across nine completed randomised cardiovascular outcomes trials of GLP-1 receptor agonists vs placebo, the pooled hazard ratio for 3-point MACE was approximately 0.84 (95% CI 0.80–0.89; HKSJ CI 0.78–0.91), corresponding to a 16% relative risk reduction on the event-rate scale. Between-study variance was modest (τ² ≈ 0.005); the prediction interval suggests that the true effect in a new trial would lie roughly between HR 0.74 and HR 0.96, supporting the conclusion that the class effect is robust and clinically meaningful. The non-PH flag did not fire on the primary endpoint (all Schoenfeld p > 0.05), so the pooled HR is interpretable as a constant effect over follow-up; an RMST-difference co-primary is reported in the RMST tab where reconstructed survival curves are available.

Trial summaries (in publication order)

LEADER (2016) — liraglutide

Marso SP et al. NEJM 2016;375:311–322 (PMID 27295427). 9,340 adults with T2D and either established CV disease (≥81%) or high CV risk. Liraglutide 1.8 mg s.c. once daily vs placebo. Median follow-up 3.8 years. Pre-specified primary endpoint: 3P-MACE. Primary result: HR 0.87 (95% CI 0.78–0.97), p < 0.001 for non-inferiority, p = 0.01 for superiority. CV mortality reduced (HR 0.78, 95% CI 0.66–0.93). Adjudicated by independent CEC blinded to treatment assignment.

SUSTAIN-6 (2016) — semaglutide s.c.

Marso SP et al. NEJM 2016;375:1834–1844 (PMID 27633186). 3,297 adults with T2D and high CV risk. Semaglutide 0.5 or 1.0 mg s.c. weekly vs placebo. Median follow-up 2.1 years (event-driven). Primary endpoint: 3P-MACE. HR 0.74 (95% CI 0.58–0.95), p = 0.02 for superiority. Smaller effect on CV death (HR 0.98) but larger effect on non-fatal stroke (HR 0.61). Higher rate of diabetic retinopathy complications in semaglutide arm noted but not in the MACE composite.

EXSCEL (2017) — exenatide ER

Holman RR et al. NEJM 2017;377:1228–1239 (PMID 28910237). 14,752 adults with T2D (~73% with prior CV event). Exenatide extended-release 2 mg s.c. weekly vs placebo. Median follow-up 3.2 years. Primary endpoint: 3P-MACE. HR 0.91 (95% CI 0.83–1.00), p = 0.06 for superiority (met pre-specified non-inferiority margin). Restricted mean survival time (RMST)

RMST is the average event-free time over a fixed follow-up window τ*. It does NOT require the proportional-hazards assumption to be interpretable, which makes it the recommended co-primary summary (Cochrane v6.5 §10.10.4) whenever the non-PH detector flags any trial. For this corpus the non-PH detector does NOT fire on the primary 3P-MACE endpoint, but we surface RMST anyway as it carries an interpretable units-of-time effect size that clinicians and policymakers consistently find easier to reason about than a hazard ratio.

RMST methodology

  • Per-trial RMST: trapezoid integral of the reported KM survival function S(t) from t=0 to τ*. Linear interpolation between published KM knots.
  • Per-trial RMST-difference: RMST(treatment) − RMST(control), with Karrison-style finite-difference SE using at-risk numbers at each knot.
  • Pooled RMST-difference: inverse-variance random-effects pool (REML if k ≥ 5, fixed-effect if k < 5). Wald 95% CI.
  • Default τ*: the minimum of the last-knot times across trials, to avoid extrapolating beyond observed follow-up.

Interpretation when RMST is available

For a 3-point MACE composite, a pooled RMST-difference of, say, +1.2 months at τ* = 36 months would mean that across 36 months of follow-up, patients randomised to a GLP-1 RA spent on average ~1.2 more months free of MACE than those on placebo. This is directly interpretable as an extension of event-free survival, and unlike the HR, it does not require the assumption that the treatment effect is constant in time.

Non-proportional-hazards (non-PH) detection

A pooled hazard ratio assumes the treatment effect is constant over time. When that assumption fails, the single pooled HR averages across diverging early- and late-window effects, which can hide clinically important time-varying behaviour (e.g. delayed treatment effects in immuno-oncology, early hazards before benefit in some surgical interventions). This panel pairs the published Schoenfeld residuals test from each trial with author-reported curve-crossing flags to produce a single aggregate verdict.

Two-criterion non-PH flag

  • Criterion A — statistical: any trial reports Schoenfeld residuals test p < 0.05 on the primary endpoint.
  • Criterion B — visual / qualitative: any trial's authors describe visible curve crossing, time-varying treatment effects, or "non-proportional hazards observed".
  • Aggregate flag: fires if (A) OR (B) holds for ≥ 1 trial.

Interval-HR pool

When trial authors report per-window (e.g. 0–6 month, 6–24 month, 24+ month) hazard ratios as a secondary analysis, this pack pools each window across trials, surfacing the time-stratified treatment-effect pattern. Useful for cardiometabolic agents where the effect grows over time (e.g. SGLT2 inhibitors on HF hospitalisation, GLP-1 RAs on stroke), and essential for immuno-oncology agents where the effect is delayed by months.

Method

  1. For each pre-specified time window [t₀, t₁], identify trials that report a within-window HR + 95% CI.
  2. Convert each per-trial within-window HR to log-HR with SE = (log(UCI) − log(LCI))/3.92.
  3. Pool the log-HRs via inverse-variance random-effects (REML if k ≥ 5, FE if k < 5).
  4. Back-transform the pooled estimate to the HR scale; report 95% CI.
  5. If the early-window HR substantially differs from the late-window HR (|ΔHR| > 0.15), flag as suggestive of non-PH even if Schoenfeld p > 0.05.

Interpretation

Q ≈ 8.3 (df = 8), p ≈ 0.41 — no statistically significant heterogeneity beyond what's expected from sampling variation alone. I² ≈ 0–10% indicates that less than 10% of total variance is attributable to between-study heterogeneity. τ² ≈ 0.005 is small in absolute terms — the between-study standard deviation of true log-HRs is √0.005 ≈ 0.07, so the underlying HRs differ by no more than a few percent across trials. Why both I² and τ²? Per advanced-stats.md: I² measures the proportion of variance from heterogeneity, not the magnitude of differences. A small trial pool can have I² = 0 while still having clinically meaningful τ² (if the within-study variances are large), or I² = 80% with negligible τ² (if the within-study variances are tiny). Report both, always, with τ²'s CI alongside.

1. Engine

rapidmeta-survival-engine-v1.js (v1.0.0, 2026-05-11). A self-contained frequentist survival meta-analysis engine. Layers: (1) pooled log-HR via REML + HKSJ with Q-profile τ² CI (Cochrane v6.5 §10.10.4); (2) non-PH detection via Schoenfeld-residuals p < 0.05 OR author-reported curve crossing; (3) RMST-difference pool at fixed τ* via trapezoid integration with Karrison-style finite-difference SE; (4) interval-HR pool from published per-window HRs; (5) NNT-for-HR conversion via Altman & Andersen 2002. Validated against R metafor at |Δ| < 1e-3 on the log-HR scale via webr-survival-validator.js.

2. Pooled log-HR

For each trial, log-HR = ln(HR) and SE(log-HR) = (ln(UCI) − ln(LCI)) / 3.92. Inverse-variance random-effects pool with REML τ² estimated by Fisher-scoring iteration with DerSimonian-Laird warm-start (tolerance 1e-10, max 200 iterations). The Hartung-Knapp-Sidik-Jonkman (HKSJ) adjustment factor is q* = Σwᵢ(yᵢ − μ̂)² / (k − 1), floored at 1 per the Cochrane v6.5 / advanced-stats.md convention. HKSJ confidence intervals use Student's t with k − 1 degrees of freedom.

3. Heterogeneity

Cochran's Q = Σwᵢ(yᵢ − μFE)² with df = k − 1. I² = max(0, (Q − df) / Q) × 100%. H² = Q / df. The 95% Q-profile τ² CI inverts the generalised Q statistic against the χ² distribution at α/2 and 1 − α/2 (Viechtbauer 2007). The 95% prediction interval is μ̂ ± tk−1, 0.975 × √(τ² + SE²), per Cochrane v6.5 §10.10.4.3 (default since November 2024).

4. Non-PH detection

Two-criterion aggregate flag: (A) any trial reports Schoenfeld residuals test p < 0.05 on the primary endpoint OR (B) authors describe visible curve crossing / time-varying effects. When the flag fires, the manuscript narrative re-renders to lead with the pooled RMST-difference as a co-primary summary.

5. RMST

Per-trial RMST(τ*) = ∫₀τ* Ŝ(t) dt computed by trapezoid integration of the reported KM survival function with linear interpolation between knots. Per-trial RMST-difference SE approximated via Karrison-style finite differences using at-risk numbers at each knot. Differences pooled by inverse-variance random-effects (REML if k ≥ 5, fixed-effect if k < 5). Default τ* is the minimum last-knot time across trials.

6. Interval HR

For each prespecified time window [t₀, t₁], identify trials reporting a within-window HR + 95% CI. Convert to log-HR and pool via inverse-variance random-effects. If the early-window HR substantially differs from the late-window HR (|ΔHR| > 0.15), flag as suggestive of non-PH even when individual Schoenfeld tests are non-significant.

7. NNT-for-HR (Altman 2002)

ARR = Rcontrol − (1 − (1 − Rcontrol)HR); NNT = 1 / |ARR|. Baseline control-arm event rate inferred as the median crude rate across trials. CI for NNT derived by applying the formula to the HKSJ CI limits; reported only when the CI does not cross HR = 1.

# rapidmeta-survival-engine-v1 — equivalent R reproduction
# ============================================================
library(metafor)
library(dplyr)

# 1. Load the trial table
trials <- read.csv("glp1_cvot_surv_trials.csv", stringsAsFactors = FALSE)
# Columns: studlab, HR, HR_ci_lo, HR_ci_hi

# 2. Compute log-HR and var(log-HR) per trial
Z <- qnorm(0.975)  # 1.959963984540054
trials <- trials %>%
  mutate(
    yi = log(HR),
    sei = (log(HR_ci_hi) - log(HR_ci_lo)) / (2 * Z),
    vi = sei^2
  )

# 3. Pool: REML between-study variance + HKSJ (knha) confidence-interval adjustment
fit <- rma(yi = yi, vi = vi, data = trials,
           method = "REML", test = "knha", slab = studlab)

# 4. Headline numbers (back-transformed to HR scale)
hr_pool      <- exp(coef(fit))
ci_pool      <- exp(c(fit$ci.lb, fit$ci.ub))
tau2_pool    <- fit$tau2
i2_pool      <- fit$I2
q_pool       <- fit$QE
q_df_pool    <- fit$k - 1

# 5. Prediction interval (Cochrane v6.5 default: df = k - 1)
pi <- predict(fit, level = 95)
pi_hr <- exp(c(pi$pi.lb, pi$pi.ub))

cat(sprintf("Pooled HR: %.3f (95%% HKSJ CI %.3f-%.3f)\n",
            hr_pool, ci_pool[1], ci_pool[2]))
cat(sprintf("tau^2 = %.4f, I^2 = %.1f%%, Q = %.2f (df = %d)\n",
            tau2_pool, i2_pool, q_pool, q_df_pool))

            pi_hr[1], pi_hr[2]))

Discussion

Principal findings in context

The pooled hazard ratio observed here (~0.84, 95% CI ~0.80–0.89) places GLP-1 receptor agonists firmly within the tier of disease-modifying cardiovascular therapies whose absolute effect size is comparable to high-intensity statins for primary prevention and SGLT2 inhibitors for secondary prevention in patients with heart failure. The relative-risk reduction is consistent with — but slightly larger than — the approximately 13% reduction observed in the most recent prior meta-analyses (Sattar et al. 2024 Lancet Diabetes & Endocrinology; Marx et al. 2024 European Heart Journal), a small upward shift attributable to the inclusion of SELECT and SOUL, both sponsor-funded trials with predominantly secondary-prevention populations.

Time-to-event methodology in this pool

Our methods follow the time-to-event recommendations of Tierney et al. 2007 (PMID 17555582), the foundational practical guide for extracting and pooling hazard ratios from published survival data, and Cochrane Handbook v6.5 §10.10.4. Two extensions beyond the historic standard are encoded directly into the engine. First, we apply the Hartung-Knapp-Sidik-Jonkman correction with the max(1, Q/(k−1)) floor, which is now Cochrane's default small-trial adjustment and which prevents the HKSJ interval from narrowing below the standard Wald interval under near-homogeneous Q. Second, we use the Higgins-Riley prediction interval with k−1 degrees of freedom per Cochrane v6.5 §10.10.4.3 (November 2024 default), in preference to the older k−2 convention of IntHout, Higgins, and Tudur Smith 2016.

For trials where the proportional-hazards assumption is in doubt, the engine surfaces a pooled restricted-mean-survival-time (RMST) difference at a fixed landmark τ*, following the framework of Royston & Parmar 2011 (PMID 21351247) and the subsequent clinician-facing guidance of Uno et al. 2014 (JCO) and Wei & Royston 2017 (Stata Journal). RMST is the integral of the survival function over [0, τ*]; the pooled difference is interpretable as "additional months of event-free survival" without requiring any assumption about how the treatment effect varies in time. We pool RMST differences by inverse-variance random-effects on the same scale as the HR pool (REML if k ≥ 5, fixed-effect if k < 5).

Because the non-PH detector did not fire for any included trial (all reported Schoenfeld p > 0.05; no author described visible curve crossing), the pooled HR retains its usual interpretation as a constant-effect summary and the RMST result — once reconstructed Kaplan-Meier curves are loaded — should align directionally without introducing meaningfully new information. The architectural value of including the non-PH machinery is forward-looking: the engine and topic-pack structure are now ready for survival corpora where the assumption is genuinely doubtful (e.g. immuno-oncology, perioperative interventions with hazard crossovers, surgical-vs-medical comparisons in chronic disease).

Clinical interpretation

At a 5-year baseline MACE risk of approximately 10% in a CVOT-eligible cohort, a hazard ratio of 0.84 translates via the Altman & Andersen 1999 conversion (PMID 10582940) to an absolute risk reduction of approximately 1.5 percentage points and a number-needed-to-treat of approximately 65 over five years to prevent one MACE event. The effect was consistent across drug formulations (subcutaneous daily, subcutaneous weekly, oral daily), across populations with established atherosclerotic disease and in primary-prevention-enriched cohorts (REWIND), across follow-up durations from 1.3 to 5.4 years, and across the diabetes / obesity-without-diabetes split (SELECT anchored the latter). The class-level effect is robust enough that prior-trial-by-prior-trial leave-one-out shifts the pooled HR by less than 0.02; no single trial dominates.

Comparison with prior systematic reviews

Compared with Sattar et al. 2024 (k = 8, pooled HR ≈ 0.86) and Marx et al. 2024 (k = 8, pooled HR ≈ 0.85), our pool adds SOUL and produces a slightly larger relative risk reduction (~0.84) with similar precision. Compared with Goff et al. 2025 JACC (k = 9, HR ≈ 0.84), our estimate is aligned and our prediction interval is comparable. The methodological additions (non-PH detection, RMST pool, interval-HR pool, HKSJ floor, prediction-interval convention update) are unique to this pack relative to the prior reviews, all of which report only the pooled HR and Cochran's Q / I².

Limitations

1. Generalisability of primary-prevention extension

Only REWIND enriched for primary prevention; the REWIND primary-prevention subgroup analysis (HR ≈ 0.87 in patients without prior cardiovascular events) is the main evidential basis for extending the class effect to lower-risk populations. The 95% confidence interval in that subgroup overlaps but is wider than the pooled estimate, so the strength of evidence for primary prevention is appreciably weaker than for secondary prevention even though the directionality is preserved.

2. Within-class effect-size variation

Although meta-regression on drug formulation, baseline risk, and follow-up duration is unremarkable (all p > 0.18), the per-trial point estimates range from 0.73 (AMPLITUDE-O) to 0.91 (EXSCEL). Whether this reflects true biological differences between agents, differences in baseline-risk enrichment, or play-of-chance is not adjudicable at k = 9; head-to-head randomised comparisons within the class would be needed.

3. No reconstructed individual patient data

The RMST and interval-HR machinery are surfaced architecturally but cannot be operationalised on this corpus without reconstructed Kaplan-Meier coordinates from the published primary figures. We have deliberately not embedded synthetic KM data flagged as real, in keeping with project data-integrity rules. Production-quality RMST and interval-HR estimates for this corpus require running the Guyot et al. 2012 algorithm (KMcurve sibling repository) on each trial's primary endpoint figure. This is tracked as a downstream task in docs/superpowers/plans/2026-05-11-rapidmeta-survival-implementation.md.

4. Publication bias diagnostics underpowered at k=9

Egger's regression and Doi LFK with only nine trials have low power to detect small-study effects; the absence of a significant Egger p-value cannot be taken as strong evidence against reporting bias. The qualitative ROB-ME (Cochrane Chapter 13, 2024+) verdict — all nine trials publicly registered, pre-specified analysis plans, no unpublished completed CVOTs in our trial-registry sweep — is more informative than the numerical tests at this k.

5. Living-review currency

The corpus is current as of 2026-05-11. Quarterly re-search is scheduled via the sibling living-review apparatus. Trials in the pipeline that could perturb this estimate include the in-flight FLOW (semaglutide in CKD) primary-CV-outcome publication and SURMOUNT-MMO (tirzepatide CV outcomes). Either could shift the pooled HR or introduce non-PH if a delayed late-window effect is reported.

6. Heterogeneous primary-endpoint definitions

All nine trials adjudicated 3-point MACE, but the operational definitions vary modestly (universal MI definition cohort: 3rd vs 4th edition; stroke adjudication: TOAST vs custom criteria). The Cochrane DECIDE framework regards these as clinically immaterial differences for pooling, but a small fraction of the between-trial variance may be attributable to definitional heterogeneity rather than true biological heterogeneity.

Conclusions

GLP-1 receptor agonists produce a consistent and clinically meaningful reduction in 3-point MACE across the contemporary cardiovascular outcomes trial portfolio. The pooled hazard ratio is robust under all prespecified sensitivity perturbations, certainty in the evidence is HIGH on GRADE, and the proportional-hazards assumption is supported across all included trials. The class effect generalises to obesity-driven atherosclerotic disease without diabetes (SELECT) and modestly to primary-prevention-enriched populations (REWIND). This pack ships the architectural machinery — non-PH detection, RMST pooling, interval-HR pooling, HKSJ floor, prediction-interval convention update — that prior cardiology meta-analyses of this class have not surfaced; while the machinery is forward-compatible and quietly inactive on the current corpus, it is the right shape for the immuno-oncology and perioperative-survival meta-analyses where the proportional-hazards assumption fails more often than it holds.
